Honestly, coding agents have changed this so much that it almost feels like a different question than it was last year. Nowadays when I land in an unfamiliar codebase, the first thing I do is just ask the agent: what's the general structure here, how do they test, how do I run this locally. In ten minutes I have a working mental model that used to take days of poking around to build.

At Eden, I did it the old way. I read the README, then found architectural docs mostly by talking to people on the team, one conversation leading to the next. I also edited some of those docs myself and asked senior engineers to comment, which turned out to be a good forcing function to check my understanding. And on my first PRs in each new area of the codebase I'd ask for a sync review instead of just async, so I could ask questions in real time rather than go back and forth in comments.
The whole point is to get the stuff that lives in people's heads, not in docs, and to avoid the mistakes the codebase tends to induce in new people.
In a new environment I'd do both: agent to get oriented fast, senior conversation to keep me from contributing wrong. 
Something I read that caught my attention was that someone created a guidelines docs to improve agent work in a new codebase and then invited a senior to discuss the doc.
