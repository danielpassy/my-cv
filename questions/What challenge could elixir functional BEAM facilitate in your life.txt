I've mostly worked with Python in a synchronous context. Whenever we needed parallelism, the solution was spinning up infrastructure: Celery with Redis or RabbitMQ as a broker, separate workers, configuring retry, backoff, deploying all of that. It's a lot of operational overhead for something that on the BEAM would just be a supervised lightweight process. The VM handles concurrency, fault isolation and retries natively, without needing external pieces.

On the functional side: I already try to apply the concept of pure functions in my day to day, even though the language doesn't enforce it. I tend to centralize state mutations in one explicit place and keep the rest of the code free of side effects. I do this because it makes the "dangerous part" of the application more obvious and the code easier to debug. But that was always my own decision, never a rule in the teams I worked in. Having that built into the language, where the compiler and the structure of the code guide you in that direction, would make everything more natural and consistent.
